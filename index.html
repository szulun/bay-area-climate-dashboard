After this habit: where “I” turns into “we”

The savings above are one household—worth doing. The Bay Area gap is still mostly shaped by infrastructure, codes, and public budgets (transit, bike lanes, reach codes, who breathes what). A habit scales when the safe lane, reliable bus, or clean building rule exists for everyone—not when guilt scales. That’s the bridge this project cares about: from a private choice to public pressure—hearings, who represents you, and ballots that fund the low-carbon default.

他山之石 = borrow ideas from peers, then adapt them. Programs actually land at city & ZIP / neighborhood scale (use Simple mode to drill down). The nine-county table below is only for orientation — the “so what” is the replicable lesson when you click a row.

Case: Berkeley — Building Emissions Saving Ordinance (BESO, rolling 2025+)

Berkeley’s BESO targets operational building emissions (assessments, upgrades, compliance paths). 2024–25 Council action added sale-tied requirements for many small (1–4 unit) homes — a concrete “existing stock” lever beyond Reach Code (which often focuses on new work). Together they show how electrification + disclosure + timing of sale can be packaged for rental & equity tradeoffs. Reach Code hub (region)

Terms: VMT = vehicle miles traveled (all driving on roads). CES = CalEnviroScreen 0–100 score (pollution + population stressors; lower is better). GHG = greenhouse gases. EJ = environmental justice.
